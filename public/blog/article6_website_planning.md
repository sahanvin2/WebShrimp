# How to Plan a Website Project: A Complete Guide

**Category:** Web Design | **Author:** Sahan Nawarathne | **Date:** February 4, 2026

---

Most website projects don't fail because of bad design or bad code.

They fail in the planning stage — or more accurately, the lack of one. Someone gets excited about a new website, jumps straight into building, and discovers six weeks later that the brief wasn't clear, the scope has ballooned, the content isn't ready, and everyone has a different idea of what "done" looks like.

I've been through enough of these to know that a well-planned website project is genuinely a joy to work on. And a poorly-planned one is stressful, expensive, and usually produces a mediocre result that nobody's proud of.

This guide walks you through exactly how we plan a website project from the very first conversation to the launch day celebration. It's based on what we've learned from building dozens of websites — the decisions that matter, the steps you can't skip, and the ones that save you hours of confusion down the road.

---

## Phase 1: Discovery — Understanding Before Building

The most important work on any website project happens before a single line of code is written or a single pixel is placed on screen.

The discovery phase is about understanding three things deeply: what the business needs, who the website is for, and what success actually looks like. It sounds obvious. You'd be surprised how often these questions get skipped in the rush to "start building."

### Define the Goals

What is this website supposed to *do*? Not what it's supposed to *look like* — what is it supposed to *accomplish*?

Every website needs measurable goals. Some examples:
- Generate 30 qualified leads per month through the contact form
- Sell products online and achieve a checkout conversion rate of at least 2%
- Establish brand credibility so prospects spend more time researching before getting in touch
- Reduce customer service requests by answering common questions through an FAQ and knowledge base

The more specific your goals, the better every subsequent decision will be. When you're debating whether to include a certain feature, you can always ask: does this help achieve our goals? If not, it doesn't belong.

### Know Your Audience

Who is actually going to be using this website? Not in a vague demographic sense — really think about it. What are they looking for? What questions do they arrive with? What do they need to see, feel, or understand before they'll take action?

Creating one or two simple audience personas helps enormously here. A persona is just a fictional but realistic description of your target user: their name, their situation, their goals, their frustrations, what they already know about you and what they don't.

When you're designing a page and you're not sure what to include, ask yourself: "What does Sarah (your persona) need to know right now to feel confident enough to take the next step?" It changes the conversation from "what do we want to say" to "what does our audience need to hear."

### Audit What Already Exists

If this is a redesign or rebuild rather than a brand new site, spend time with the current website before rushing to replace it. Look at your analytics: which pages get the most traffic? Which pages have the highest exit rates? What content are people actually reading? What's the current site missing that users are clearly looking for?

Google Analytics, Google Search Console, and tools like Hotjar (which records user sessions and heatmaps) can tell you an enormous amount about how people currently interact with your site. Don't throw away what's working.

## Phase 2: Strategy — Making Key Decisions

With a clear understanding of the goals, audience, and existing situation, you can make the important strategic decisions before you're too deep to change them.

### Sitemap and Information Architecture

A sitemap is a simple diagram showing all the pages on your website and how they relate to each other. Before you design anything, you need to know what pages exist and how users will navigate between them.

Start with the main sections (Home, About, Services, Blog, Contact) and then map out sub-pages where needed. Think about the user's journey: what pages will they visit first, and what do you want them to do next? Every page should lead somewhere — to a contact form, a product page, a related article, a booking system.

Keep the navigation simple. People should be able to find anything on your site within two or three clicks. If you find yourself building a seven-item navigation with multiple levels of dropdowns, it's time to simplify.

### Decide on the Technology Stack

What platform will the website be built on? This decision should be based on your project's specific needs, not on trends or what your designer happens to prefer.

Some questions to guide the decision:
- Does the client need to update content regularly? (If yes, you need a CMS — WordPress, Webflow, or similar)
- Is there e-commerce functionality? (Shopify or WooCommerce are the most battle-tested options)
- Is the website primarily a marketing site or a complex web application? (Marketing sites: WordPress or Webflow. Applications: a custom framework like Next.js + Laravel)
- What's the hosting budget? (Some platforms have significant monthly costs)
- How technical is the client team that will maintain it? (Webflow is more intuitive for non-technical users; WordPress requires a bit more familiarity)

Get this decision right before you start building. Changing platforms midway through a project is expensive and painful.

### Content Planning

This is the step that gets skipped most often, and it's the reason so many website projects stall halfway through.

Here's the painful truth: you cannot design a website without content. Every layout decision depends on the content it's supposed to hold. How long is the headline? How many services do you offer? Do you have real photography or are we working with stock? Are there testimonials ready, or do we need to collect them?

Create a content matrix early — a simple spreadsheet that lists every page, every section on each page, and who is responsible for providing the content by what date. Get sign-off on this before anyone starts designing anything.

If photography is needed, book the photographer now. Good photographers have lead times of four to six weeks. Don't discover this fact three weeks before your launch date.

## Phase 3: Design — From Concept to Approved Visuals

With strategy locked in, the design phase can begin — and it should follow a clear, structured process rather than jumping straight into full mockups.

### Wireframes First

Wireframes are low-fidelity layouts that show the structure and content hierarchy of each page without any visual design applied. They're usually gray boxes and placeholder text — deliberately rough.

Don't skip this step. Wireframes are cheap to change. A full-color, pixel-perfect mockup is expensive to change. Getting alignment on structure, content placement, and user flow at the wireframe stage saves enormous amounts of time later.

Present wireframes to your client or stakeholder and walk through them page by page. Ask: does this layout tell the right story? Is the most important information prominent enough? Is the navigation logical? Is the CTA clear?

### Visual Design

Once wireframes are approved, the visual design phase begins. This is where typography, color, photography, icons, and visual hierarchy come together to create the look and feel of the site.

Best practice: design the homepage and one or two inner templates first before designing every page. Get feedback and approval on these before completing the rest. The design language established in these early pages carries through everything else — if you need to make a significant style change, you want to catch it when you've only designed two pages, not twenty.

Present designs in context. Show how the homepage looks on a laptop, how a service page looks on an iPhone, how the navigation works on a tablet. Design doesn't exist in a vacuum — it needs to be seen in the environment where it'll actually be used.

### Design Review and Approval

Before moving to development, get formal sign-off on the final designs. This means written confirmation (an email is fine) that the designs are approved and ready to build. This protects everyone — it sets a clear milestone, and it means that if significant design changes are requested after development has started, both parties understand that this affects scope and timeline.

## Phase 4: Development — Building the Real Thing

Development is where the approved designs become a functioning website. Here's how to manage this phase well.

### Set Up a Staging Environment

Build the website on a staging URL (something like staging.yourwebsite.com) — a private environment where the development happens before anything is made live. This lets the development team build and test without interfering with your existing live website.

### Build in Sprints

Rather than building everything and then doing one big review at the end, review the work in stages. Review the homepage first, then the core inner pages, then the functional elements (contact forms, e-commerce checkout, blog). Catching issues early is always faster and less expensive than catching them late.

### Cross-Browser and Device Testing

Test on multiple browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge) and multiple device sizes. What looks perfect in Chrome on a 15-inch MacBook can look broken in Safari on an iPhone 12 if you're not careful. This testing should happen throughout development, not just at the very end.

## Phase 5: Pre-Launch Checklist

Before going live, run through every item on this checklist:

**Content:**
- All placeholder text replaced with real content
- All images optimized and have alt text
- All links tested and working (no 404s)
- Contact forms tested and routing to the correct email

**SEO:**
- Page titles and meta descriptions set for every page
- Google Analytics and Google Search Console connected
- XML sitemap generated and submitted
- Robots.txt configured correctly

**Performance:**
- PageSpeed Insights score acceptable on mobile and desktop
- Images in WebP format or compressed appropriately
- Caching configured

**Security:**
- SSL certificate installed (HTTPS, not HTTP)
- Admin credentials strong and stored safely
- CMS and plugins updated to latest versions

**Legal:**
- Privacy policy page present (required by GDPR and equivalent laws)
- Cookie consent if applicable
- Terms of service if relevant

---

## Phase 6: Launch and Beyond

On launch day, do it deliberately. Set a specific time window, make sure the right people are available, and have a rollback plan if something goes wrong. Monitor the site closely for the first 24–48 hours.

After launch, set a reminder to review your goals against your analytics in 30, 60, and 90 days. A website isn't a one-time project — it's a living platform that should improve over time as you learn how your users interact with it.

The best websites aren't the ones that were built perfectly the first time. They're the ones with teams behind them that keep paying attention, keep improving, and keep making decisions based on real data.

---

## The Short Version

Good website projects follow a clear sequence: understand before you build, decide before you design, design before you develop. Skip any of those steps and you'll pay for it later — in scope creep, in rework, in a website that nobody's quite happy with.

Take the planning seriously. It's not the exciting part, but it's the part that determines whether everything else goes well.
